## Corpus synthesis

- Median core length: **9,442 words**. Interquartile range: **8,296 to 10,664**. Range: **6,666 to 12,598**.
- Median published length: **22 pages** in the PACM ISS format.
- Typical top-level budgets: introduction **760 words**, related work/background **1,170**, each major method/design/study block **1,629**, results/findings **1,988**, discussion/implications **1,076**, limitations **467**, conclusion **183**.
- Median visual load: **10.5 figures and 1 table**, or about **14 numbered figures/tables per 10,000 core words**.
- Seventeen papers use appendices or supplementary material substantively. They move derivations, code, complete themes/codes, detailed statistics, timelines, extra study results, and videos out of the narrative.
- Sentence style is not uniformly terse. The median paper averages about **27 words per sentence**, but successful papers keep rhetorical jobs distinct: problem, gap, artifact/method, evidence, and implication.
- Hedging is usually selective rather than constant. The median is about **7 hedge markers per 1,000 words**. Strong papers state the observed direction directly, then name the boundary once.

### Typical contribution framing

Across the set, the common abstract sequence is: concrete problem, specific system or method, study/sample, primary result, bounded implication. The intro usually ends with one canonical numbered list of two to four contributions. Common verbs are **introduce, present, propose, develop, evaluate, demonstrate, investigate, provide**, and **derive**. Novelty is normally visible before the end of the introduction, frequently on page 2 or 3 of the published article.

## High-relevance papers and citation gaps

### 3698142.pdf: Evaluating Typing Performance in Different Mixed Reality Manifestations using Physiological Features

**Why it is high relevance.** This is the corpus paper closest to the physiological-computing side of the submission. It measures ECG, EEG, and eye tracking in AR, augmented virtuality, and VR, and explicitly connects physiological features to adaptive MR.

**Methods and findings to engage with.**

- ECG was collected with a Polar H10 at 130 Hz, processed with NeuroKit, filtered at 3 to 45 Hz, normalized to a resting condition, and reduced to HR for the reported study. This is a useful methodological comparison point, not a protocol to copy unless the hardware and preprocessing match.
- The paper says common time-domain measures such as HR and HRV have not consistently discriminated workload states. It then reports lower HR in augmented virtuality and VR than AR and interprets that pattern cautiously as reduced stress or greater relaxation.
- The paper uses physiology as one evidential stream beside behavior, self-report, EEG, and gaze. That supports the submission's argument against treating one biosignal as a complete or transparent state label.
- Its adaptive-interface discussion distinguishes sensing a physiological correlate from deciding how a system should act on that correlate. That distinction fits the submission's STS claim that measurement and classification help constitute the phenomenon.

**Citations worth checking during Stage 2.**

- Nickel and Nachreiner (2003), *Sensitivity and diagnosticity of the 0.1-Hz component of heart rate variability as an indicator of mental workload*.
- Pohl and Murray-Smith (2013), *Focused and casual interactions: allowing users to vary their level of engagement*.
- Lindlbauer, Feit, and Hilliges (2019), *Context-Aware Online Adaptation of Mixed Reality Interfaces*.
- Mayer, Chiossi, and Mayer (2024), *Crossing Mixed Realities: A Review for Transitional Interfaces Design*.
- Prinzel et al. (2003), work on EEG, event-related potentials, and HRV for adaptive automation.

**Current gap.** The paper itself is absent from both **sample-base.bib** and **software.bib**. It belongs in the physiological computing or measurement-related-work subsection. It should be used both as recent ISS precedent and as a caution: its study analyzes HR, not HRV, and it explicitly notes inconsistent interpretation of HR/HRV as workload markers.

### 3698148.pdf: There Is More to Avatars Than Visuals

**Why it is high relevance.** This is the corpus paper closest to the submission's remote teammate, avatar, coordination, attention, and performance problem.

**Methods and findings to engage with.**

- It uses a 2 by 2 within-subject design with 48 participants in 24 dyads: Simple versus Rich Avatar, crossed with Mono versus Spatial Audio.
- It combines UEQ-S, NASA-TLX, Networked Minds social presence, task completion, gaze to the avatar/head, proxemic distance, preference rankings, and thematic analysis. That multimethod structure is a strong comparison for separating legibility, experience, and coordination outcomes.
- Rich avatars improved hedonic experience and drew more gaze to the head. Participants described simple avatars as less distracting and sometimes better suited to performance-oriented work. Visual richness did not simply produce better task performance.
- Spatial audio supported faster parallel-task completion. Social presence was high across conditions rather than cleanly determined by richer visuals.
- The paper names the trade-off once and turns it into a design perspective: richer representation for social/creative situations, reduced visual representation where focused task performance matters. This is a close rhetorical precedent for the submission's honest legibility-versus-precision trade-off.

**Citations worth checking during Stage 2.**

- Seinfeld et al. (2021), *User Representations in Human-Computer Interaction*.
- Weidner et al. (2023), systematic review of avatar and agent visualization in AR/VR HMDs.
- Yang et al. (2020), effects of spatial auditory and visual cues on MR remote collaboration.
- Yoon et al. (2019), avatar appearance and social presence in AR remote collaboration.
- Fink et al. (2022), remote collaboration in incongruent personal/shared spaces.

**Current gap.** The paper itself is absent from both bibliography files. It belongs in the remote-collaboration/avatar related work and should also be revisited in the discussion when positioning the submission's trade-off. It is more current and more directly comparable than a generic avatar citation.

### Safe claim templates

Adapt these to the manuscript's voice. Do not copy them blindly, and do not imply stronger evidence than the papers provide.

- **Physiological measurement:** Recent ISS work has combined ECG with EEG and gaze to characterize engagement and workload across mixed-reality conditions, while also noting that common HR and HRV measures do not consistently discriminate workload states [cite 3698142 and its cited psychophysiology sources].
- **Measurement caution:** Chiossi et al. analyze HR rather than HRV in their experiment, so the paper supports a multimethod and context-sensitive interpretation of physiology, not a claim that HRV transparently identifies a teammate's state [cite 3698142].
- **Avatar richness trade-off:** In remote AR collaboration, richer avatar representation increased hedonic quality and attention to the avatar's head, while simpler representation was described as less distracting and did not straightforwardly reduce task performance [cite 3698148].
- **Coordination channels:** Fink et al. show that visual and auditory representation channels can have different consequences for attention, experience, and parallel-task completion, which motivates evaluating compositional channels separately [cite 3698148].

### Do not overclaim

- **3698142.pdf** does not validate the submission's HRV encoding, does not display physiology to a teammate, and does not test social interpretation of biosignals.
- **3698148.pdf** does not use physiological data, does not test HR or HRV, and does not evaluate an ambiguous biosignal icon.
- Neither paper establishes the submission's numerical findings, its 3.9-second effect, its effect size, or the validity of its forced-choice task. Those claims must come only from the submission's own verified data.

## Integrity notes

- The two split files for **3626480.pdf** were not double-counted.
- Appendix material was excluded from core counts even when it appeared before the reference list.
- Four PDFs emitted malformed numeric-object warnings during extraction. Their text remained readable and their first pages rendered correctly. No paper failed extraction.
- Rotated text in several figures and tables is not fully represented in extracted prose. Caption counts and rendered-page checks were used to avoid treating that omission as missing paper content.
- The corpus contains no paper that directly tests an HR-plus-HRV compositional avatar encoding or a chance-referenced forced-choice test of an ambiguous biosignal display. Those remain credible novelty claims, subject to broader literature verification beyond these 48 papers.
- Stage 2 has not been performed. The only manuscript-side check in this stage was confirming whether the two high-relevance papers already appear in the bibliography. No source was edited.
